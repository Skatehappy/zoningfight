Special Use Permit Appeal Law in Florida

In Florida, special use permits (sometimes called special exceptions or conditional use permits) are land use approvals issued by local zoning boards, planning commissions, or city or county commissions under authority granted by Chapter 163, Florida Statutes, and local land development codes. Most special use permit decisions are considered quasi-judicial because they apply existing standards to specific facts, rather than creating new legislation. The Florida Supreme Court's decision in Board of County Commissioners of Brevard County v. Snyder, 627 So. 2d 469 (Fla. 1993), established that applicants must show their request is consistent with the comprehensive plan and meets the criteria in the local code. Once that showing is made, the burden shifts to the local government to demonstrate, by competent substantial evidence, that denial serves a legitimate public purpose. Aggrieved parties — including applicants and neighbors with standing — may seek review by petition for writ of certiorari in circuit court under Florida Rule of Appellate Procedure 9.190. The reviewing court asks three questions: whether procedural due process was afforded, whether the essential requirements of law were observed, and whether the decision is supported by competent substantial evidence. The court does not reweigh evidence or substitute its judgment. Separately, Fla. Stat. § 163.3215 provides a cause of action for development orders inconsistent with a local comprehensive plan, requiring a verified complaint and a 30-day pre-suit notice to the local government. Local codes may also provide an internal administrative appeal to a board of adjustment before judicial review is available, and exhaustion of those remedies is generally required.

How a Demand Letter Works in Florida

A demand letter in a Florida special use permit dispute serves several strategic purposes. First, it formally notifies the local government, board members, and any opposing applicant of your legal position before you file a costly certiorari petition. Second, it satisfies the 30-day pre-suit notice required under Fla. Stat. § 163.3215(4) for comprehensive plan consistency challenges. Third, it creates a written record that can support later claims for attorney's fees, equitable estoppel, or Bert J. Harris Act relief. Your letter should identify the specific permit decision and date of rendition, explain why the decision lacks competent substantial evidence or violates the local code or comprehensive plan, cite controlling Florida cases like Snyder and Jennings v. Dade County, and demand a specific remedy — rehearing, withdrawal of conditions, or rescission. It should also preserve all available legal theories, including procedural due process violations, ex parte communications, and inconsistency with the comprehensive plan. Including a clear deadline for response, typically 10 to 14 days, encourages prompt engagement. Many Florida local governments will reconsider, schedule a rehearing, or negotiate revised conditions when faced with a credible, well-supported letter, especially where ex parte contacts, inadequate findings, or insufficient evidence are documented. Even if the matter proceeds to circuit court, the letter narrows issues, locks in admissions, and demonstrates good faith — factors judges consider when evaluating fee awards and equitable remedies.

Procedural Notes for Florida

Certiorari petitions challenging quasi-judicial zoning decisions must be filed in the circuit court of the county where the property is located within 30 days of rendition of the written order. Filing fees in Florida circuit court are typically around $401, though they vary by county. A comprehensive plan consistency action under Fla. Stat. § 163.3215 requires a verified complaint and a 30-day pre-suit notice. Florida's small claims jurisdiction (currently $8,000 or less) does not apply to zoning appeals, which are equitable and statutory in nature. Exhaustion of local administrative remedies, such as appeal to a board of adjustment, is generally required before judicial review. Procedures vary by jurisdiction, so always consult the local land development code.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to appeal a special use permit decision in Florida?
You generally have 30 days from the date the written order is rendered to file a petition for writ of certiorari in circuit court under Florida Rule of Appellate Procedure 9.190. 'Rendition' usually means when the signed written order is filed with the clerk, not the date of the public hearing vote. Missing this deadline almost always forfeits your right to judicial review, so it is critical to act quickly and confirm the rendition date with the local government clerk.
Do I have standing to challenge a neighbor's special use permit?
Florida courts allow adjacent or nearby property owners to challenge special use permit decisions if they can show a special injury different in kind from the general public. Typical examples include increased traffic, noise, drainage problems, loss of property value, or impacts on use and enjoyment. Standing under Fla. Stat. § 163.3215 for comprehensive plan consistency challenges is broader and includes any 'aggrieved or adversely affected party.' Documenting your specific harm with photos, appraisals, or expert opinions strengthens both standing and the merits.
What is 'competent substantial evidence' in a Florida zoning appeal?
Competent substantial evidence is evidence a reasonable mind would accept as adequate to support a conclusion. In special use permit cases, it must come from the hearing record — sworn testimony, expert reports, staff analyses, traffic or environmental studies, and authenticated documents. Generalized neighborhood opposition, fear, or speculation does not qualify. If a local board denied or approved a permit based only on unsupported lay objections, that decision is vulnerable on certiorari review for lacking competent substantial evidence.
Can I recover attorney's fees if I win my Florida special use permit appeal?
Fee recovery is limited but possible. Fla. Stat. § 163.3215(6) authorizes prevailing party fees in comprehensive plan consistency actions in some circumstances. Some local codes also contain fee-shifting provisions. In straight certiorari proceedings, fees are generally not awarded unless a contract, statute, or rule specifically authorizes them or the opposing party acted in bad faith. Discuss fee exposure and recovery with a Florida land use attorney before filing, because you can also be ordered to pay fees if you lose certain claims.
Should I send a demand letter before filing a certiorari petition?
Yes, in most cases. A demand letter is required before filing a Fla. Stat. § 163.3215 comprehensive plan action, and it is strongly recommended before any certiorari petition. It can prompt the local government to schedule a rehearing, modify conditions, or correct procedural errors without litigation. Even if it does not resolve the dispute, it preserves your record, demonstrates good faith, narrows the issues, and may support later claims for fees or equitable relief. Always send it well before the 30-day certiorari deadline.
