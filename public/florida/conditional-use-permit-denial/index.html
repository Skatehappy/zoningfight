Conditional Use Permit Denial Law in Florida

Florida law distinguishes between legislative zoning decisions and quasi-judicial decisions. Conditional use permit applications fall squarely into the quasi-judicial category under the landmark case Board of County Commissioners v. Snyder, 627 So. 2d 469 (Fla. 1993). This means a local zoning board, planning commission, or city council acts like a court when ruling on your CUP—it must hold a fair hearing, allow sworn testimony and cross-examination, and base its decision on competent substantial evidence. Mere neighborhood opposition, traffic generalizations, or aesthetic objections without expert support do not qualify as competent substantial evidence. Under Fla. Stat. § 166.041 (municipalities) and § 125.66 (counties), local governments must follow strict notice and hearing procedures. If the applicant demonstrates compliance with the published criteria for a conditional use, the burden shifts to the local government to show, with substantial evidence, that the proposed use conflicts with the comprehensive plan or zoning code. Review of a CUP denial is by petition for writ of certiorari filed in the circuit court under Florida Rule of Appellate Procedure 9.190 and 9.100. The reviewing court asks three questions: (1) was due process afforded; (2) did the board observe essential requirements of law; and (3) is the decision supported by competent substantial evidence. The court does not reweigh evidence but will quash a denial that fails any prong. Separately, Fla. Stat. § 163.3215 provides a cause of action for development orders inconsistent with the local comprehensive plan, with its own procedural requirements including a verified complaint and pre-suit notice.

How a Demand Letter Works in Florida

A Florida CUP denial demand letter serves three strategic purposes. First, it preserves the record by formally identifying the legal and factual deficiencies in the board's decision—missing findings of fact, reliance on lay opinion testimony, or failure to apply the published code criteria. Second, it creates leverage by signaling that the applicant is prepared to file a certiorari petition within the 30-day window, which exposes the local government to reversal, remand, and potential fee-shifting under § 57.105 if the denial lacked any reasonable basis in law or fact. Third, it opens a pathway to settlement, including reconsideration at a subsequent hearing, modified conditions of approval, or a stipulated remand. Effective letters cite the specific provisions of the local land development code the applicant satisfied, reference the Snyder burden-shifting framework, identify by name the witnesses or evidence the board improperly relied upon (such as unsworn neighbor comments), and attach a draft petition for writ of certiorari. The letter should be addressed to the city or county attorney, with copies to the clerk and the chair of the deciding body. Including a clear deadline for response—typically 10 to 14 days—keeps pressure aligned with the 30-day certiorari clock. Many Florida local governments will reopen the record or schedule a rehearing rather than defend a weak denial in circuit court, particularly where the written order lacks adequate findings of fact tying the decision to specific code criteria.

Procedural Notes for Florida

The 30-day deadline runs from rendition of the written order, not the oral vote. Rendition occurs when a signed, written order is filed with the clerk. Petitions for writ of certiorari are filed in the circuit court of the county where the property sits; filing fees are typically $401. Florida small claims court (limit $8,000) cannot hear CUP appeals—certiorari is the exclusive remedy. Some jurisdictions require exhaustion of administrative appeals to a board of adjustment before circuit court review; check the local code. Section 163.3215 consistency challenges require a verified complaint filed within 30 days and a pre-suit notice to the local government. Attorney's fees may be available under § 57.105 for frivolous denials, but recovery is rare and fact-dependent.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to challenge a CUP denial in Florida?
You have 30 days from rendition of the written denial order to file a petition for writ of certiorari in circuit court. Rendition means the signed written order is filed with the clerk, not the date of the oral vote. This deadline is jurisdictional—miss it and the court cannot hear your case. Sending a demand letter early in this window preserves your position while leaving time to file if the local government refuses to reconsider.
What is 'competent substantial evidence' in a Florida zoning case?
Competent substantial evidence is relevant evidence a reasonable mind would accept as adequate to support a conclusion. In CUP cases, it typically means expert testimony from planners, traffic engineers, or appraisers, or specific factual data tied to the code criteria. Generalized neighbor complaints about traffic, noise, or property values—without expert support or specific facts—do not qualify. If the board's denial rests only on lay opinion or speculation, a Florida circuit court will quash the decision on certiorari review.
Can I sue in small claims court for a CUP denial?
No. Florida small claims court has an $8,000 jurisdictional limit and only hears civil money disputes. CUP denials must be challenged through a petition for writ of certiorari filed in the circuit court of the county where the property is located, under Florida Rule of Appellate Procedure 9.190. The circuit court reviews the record from the local hearing and decides whether to quash the denial. Damages claims are generally unavailable except in narrow inverse condemnation situations.
Do I have to exhaust local appeals before going to court?
It depends on the local code. Many Florida cities and counties require an applicant to appeal a staff or planning commission denial to a board of adjustment or city council before seeking certiorari review. Failure to exhaust these administrative remedies can result in dismissal of your petition. Review the specific land development code for your jurisdiction, and reference any administrative appeal rights in your demand letter to preserve them while the 30-day clock runs.
Can I recover attorney's fees if I win my appeal?
Sometimes. Florida Statute § 57.105 allows fee recovery when a party knew or should have known its position lacked any basis in law or fact. Recovery against a local government for a CUP denial is possible but uncommon, because courts give zoning bodies wide latitude. Section 163.3215 consistency challenges have separate fee provisions. Your demand letter should preserve the fee claim by citing § 57.105 and identifying the specific deficiencies that make the denial legally unsupportable.
