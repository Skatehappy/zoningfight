Nonconforming Use Letter Law in California

California recognizes legal nonconforming uses under both common law vested rights doctrine and statutory provisions in the Planning and Zoning Law (Gov. Code §§ 65000 et seq.). A nonconforming use exists when a property's use was lawfully established before a zoning change made that use no longer permitted. The landmark case Hansen Brothers Enterprises v. Board of Supervisors (1996) 12 Cal.4th 533 confirms that owners have a constitutionally protected right to continue such uses, subject to reasonable regulation.

Local agencies may regulate nonconforming uses through amortization (a phased termination over a reasonable period), restrictions on expansion, or termination upon abandonment. Under California law, abandonment generally requires both (1) an intent to abandon and (2) an overt act or failure to act demonstrating that intent—mere cessation of use for a period, without intent, is typically insufficient absent a clear ordinance provision.

Government Code § 65852.25 specifically addresses nonconforming residential uses and limits how cities may force conversion. Cities may impose amortization schedules, but these must be reasonable and provide adequate time to recoup investment, per County of Los Angeles v. Gage (1954) 127 Cal.App.2d 442.

Key defenses for property owners include: (1) proof the use was lawful when established; (2) continuous use without abandonment; (3) no unlawful expansion or intensification; and (4) compliance with any registration or certificate-of-occupancy requirements. Documentation such as old permits, business licenses, tax records, aerial photographs, utility bills, and sworn declarations from longtime neighbors can establish the use's pre-ordinance origin. Cities bear the burden of proving termination once the owner shows lawful establishment.

How a Demand Letter Works in California

A California Nonconforming Use Letter serves three strategic purposes: it creates a written record, triggers administrative review, and preserves judicial remedies. Send the letter to the planning director, code enforcement officer, and city attorney by certified mail with return receipt requested.

The letter should: (1) identify the property by APN and address; (2) describe the existing use and its history; (3) cite the date the use was established and the date the conflicting ordinance was adopted; (4) attach documentary evidence such as permits, licenses, photos, and declarations; (5) cite Gov. Code §§ 65852.25 and 65009 plus Hansen Brothers Enterprises; (6) demand withdrawal of any notice of violation, abatement order, or citation; and (7) reserve all rights including a § 65009 challenge and inverse condemnation claims.

In California, the letter often resolves disputes before formal hearings because planning staff must verify their legal position under tight 90-day litigation deadlines. If the city refuses, the letter becomes critical evidence of exhaustion of administrative remedies—a prerequisite to filing a writ of mandate under Code of Civil Procedure § 1094.5.

A strong letter also positions the owner for attorney's fees under CCP § 1021.5 if the matter proceeds to court and vindicates an important public right. Tone should be firm but professional; avoid threats and focus on factual documentation. Request a written response within 15-30 days and a meeting with planning staff to walk through the evidence.

Procedural Notes for California

California imposes a strict 90-day statute of limitations under Gov. Code § 65009(c) to challenge most zoning and land use decisions—missing this deadline typically forfeits judicial review. Writ petitions are filed in superior court; filing fees are approximately $450 plus a CEQA-related fee where applicable. Small claims court (limit $12,500 for individuals) is generally unsuitable for zoning disputes because injunctive and declaratory relief are unavailable there. Administrative exhaustion is mandatory: pursue the planning commission appeal and city council review before filing in court. Charter cities may have shorter local appeal windows (often 10-15 days), so check the municipal code. Government Tort Claims Act notice (Gov. Code § 910) may apply if damages are sought.

Frequently Asked Questions

What qualifies as a legal nonconforming use in California?
A use qualifies if it was lawfully established under the zoning rules in effect at the time, then later became noncompliant due to a zoning change. You must show the use existed continuously, was permitted when started, and has not been abandoned, unlawfully expanded, or substantially altered. Evidence typically includes old permits, business licenses, tax assessments, photographs, and declarations from neighbors or prior owners documenting the use's history.
How long do I have to challenge a zoning enforcement action?
Under California Government Code § 65009(c), you generally have only 90 days from the date the agency's decision becomes final to file a writ petition in superior court. Some cities impose shorter administrative appeal deadlines—often 10 to 15 days—to appeal to the planning commission or city council. Missing either deadline can permanently bar your challenge, so send your nonconforming use letter immediately and calendar all appeal dates.
Can a California city force me to stop a nonconforming use?
Cities may terminate nonconforming uses through reasonable amortization periods that allow owners to recoup investment, but the schedule must be reasonable under County of Los Angeles v. Gage. Termination is also possible upon proven abandonment, illegal expansion, or destruction beyond a threshold (often 50%). However, cities cannot arbitrarily revoke vested rights without due process, and abrupt shutdowns may constitute a regulatory taking requiring compensation.
Do I need to register my nonconforming use with the city?
Some California jurisdictions require nonconforming use certificates or registration; others do not. Failing to register where required can jeopardize your protected status. Check your local zoning code for any 'certificate of nonconformity' or 'legal nonconforming use permit' provisions, and request one in writing if available. Registration creates an official record that strengthens your position in any future enforcement dispute.
Will a nonconforming use letter avoid going to court?
Often yes. A well-documented letter citing California statutes and case law frequently prompts planning staff to withdraw violation notices, especially when faced with the 90-day litigation clock and potential attorney's fees under CCP § 1021.5. Even when it does not fully resolve the dispute, the letter establishes your administrative exhaustion, preserves your defenses, and creates a contemporaneous evidentiary record that strengthens any later writ petition or inverse condemnation claim.
