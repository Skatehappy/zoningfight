Setback Variance Request Law in Arizona

Arizona zoning variances are governed primarily by A.R.S. § 9-462.06 for properties inside city or town limits and A.R.S. § 11-816 for unincorporated county land. Both statutes empower a Board of Adjustment to grant variances from strict zoning requirements—including front, side, and rear setbacks—when an applicant demonstrates specific legal criteria. Under Arizona law, a variance may only be granted when: (1) special circumstances apply to the property, such as size, shape, topography, or location, that do not apply to other properties in the same zoning district; (2) strict enforcement of the setback would deprive the property owner of privileges enjoyed by other property in the same classification; and (3) the variance would not constitute a special privilege inconsistent with the limitations on other properties in the same zone. Arizona courts have consistently held that economic hardship alone is not enough. The hardship must be tied to the physical characteristics of the land itself, not personal circumstances or financial preference. The Arizona Supreme Court in Ivancovich v. City of Tucson Board of Adjustment (1973) made clear that variances are an extraordinary remedy and the standard is strict. Self-created hardships—where the owner caused the problem by their own actions—are generally disqualifying. Each Arizona municipality (Phoenix, Tucson, Scottsdale, Mesa, Flagstaff, and others) has its own zoning code that supplements state law, often adding application requirements, notice rules, and hearing procedures. Counties like Maricopa, Pima, and Coconino follow § 11-816 and county-specific ordinances. A use variance—changing the permitted use of land—is generally not allowed in Arizona; only area variances like setbacks are typically available.

How a Demand Letter Works in Arizona

A strong setback variance request letter in Arizona does more than ask for permission—it builds the legal record. Start by identifying the property with its full legal description, parcel number, and current zoning classification. Cite the specific setback requirement in the local zoning code and identify exactly how much relief you need (for example, a reduction from 25 feet to 15 feet on the rear setback). Then walk through the three Arizona variance criteria in order, applying the facts of your property to each. Describe the special circumstances—an unusually narrow lot, a wash or drainage easement, a slope, mature heritage trees, or a pre-existing nonconforming structure. Explain how strict enforcement deprives you of rights enjoyed by neighbors, ideally with examples of nearby properties. Address why granting the variance is not a special privilege—often by showing the request is the minimum necessary and consistent with the neighborhood's character. Attach supporting documentation: a site plan, survey, photographs, and any letters of support from adjacent property owners. Reference A.R.S. § 9-462.06 or § 11-816 directly. Send the letter to the zoning administrator and copy the Board of Adjustment clerk. A professional, fact-driven letter often resolves the matter at the staff level or sets up a successful Board hearing, and it preserves your right to appeal to superior court via special action if denied.

Procedural Notes for Arizona

Arizona variance applications are filed with the local planning or zoning department, not the courts. Filing fees vary widely by jurisdiction—typically $300 to $1,500—and most cities require a pre-application meeting. Public notice and a hearing before the Board of Adjustment are mandatory, with notice usually mailed to property owners within 300 feet. You generally have 30 days from a zoning administrator's decision to appeal to the Board, and 30 days from the Board's final decision to file a special action in Arizona Superior Court under A.R.S. § 12-2101 and Rule 4, Arizona Rules of Procedure for Special Actions. Small claims court (limit $3,500) does not handle zoning matters. Deadlines are jurisdictional—missing them typically forfeits your rights.

Frequently Asked Questions

What qualifies as a 'hardship' for a setback variance in Arizona?
Under Arizona law, the hardship must come from the physical features of your property—an irregular shape, steep slope, narrow width, washes, or other unique conditions—not from personal or financial reasons. Wanting a bigger garage or saving construction costs is not enough. The Arizona Supreme Court has made clear that variances are an extraordinary remedy. You must show special circumstances that don't apply to neighboring properties in the same zoning district and that strict enforcement would deny you rights other owners enjoy.
How long does the Arizona variance process take?
Most Arizona jurisdictions process setback variance requests in 60 to 120 days from a complete application. The timeline includes staff review, public notice (typically 15 days before hearing), the Board of Adjustment hearing, and any post-decision appeal period. Larger cities like Phoenix and Tucson may take longer due to backlog. If denied, you have 30 days to file a special action in superior court. Sending a well-prepared variance request letter early can shorten the process by reducing staff requests for additional information.
Can I appeal if my variance is denied?
Yes. Under A.R.S. § 12-2101 and Arizona's special action rules, you can challenge a Board of Adjustment decision in superior court within 30 days of the final ruling. The court reviews whether the Board acted arbitrarily, exceeded its jurisdiction, or abused its discretion—not whether it made the 'right' decision. Because review is limited, building a strong factual record at the Board hearing, supported by your variance request letter and exhibits, is essential. Most successful appeals turn on procedural errors or lack of substantial evidence.
Do I need a lawyer to request a setback variance in Arizona?
No, Arizona law does not require a lawyer to file a variance application or appear before the Board of Adjustment. Many homeowners successfully handle simple setback requests themselves, especially with a well-drafted letter that addresses the three legal criteria. However, if your project is complex, neighbors are opposing, or you anticipate appealing to superior court, hiring a land use attorney is wise. A lawyer can also help if a denial threatens significant property value or development plans.
What's the difference between a variance and a zoning amendment in Arizona?
A variance grants relief from a specific standard (like a setback) for a single property based on hardship, decided by the Board of Adjustment. A zoning amendment—or rezoning—changes the underlying zoning classification or text of the ordinance and is decided by the city council or county board of supervisors after a Planning Commission recommendation. Variances are faster and cheaper but limited in scope. Arizona generally prohibits 'use variances,' so if you need to change the allowed use of land, you must pursue rezoning instead.
