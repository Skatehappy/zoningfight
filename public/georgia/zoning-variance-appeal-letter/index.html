Zoning Variance Appeal Letter Law in Georgia

Georgia regulates zoning variances through a combination of state statutes and local ordinances. The Zoning Procedures Law, codified at O.C.G.A. § 36-66-1 through § 36-66-9, requires every county and municipality to adopt written procedures for hearing zoning matters, including variances, special use permits, and rezonings. Each local government must publish notice, hold a public hearing, and issue a written decision based on adopted standards.

Under Georgia law, a variance is typically granted only when the applicant demonstrates: (1) extraordinary and exceptional conditions pertaining to the property because of size, shape, or topography; (2) the application of the zoning ordinance would create an unnecessary hardship; (3) the conditions are peculiar to the particular property; and (4) granting the variance would not cause substantial detriment to the public good or impair the purposes of the zoning ordinance. These four factors, often called the "hardship test," come from decades of Georgia appellate decisions including *City of Atlanta v. Wansley Moving & Storage Co.* and similar rulings.

If the BZA denies a variance or grants one over your objection, Georgia provides judicial review by writ of certiorari to the Superior Court of the county where the property sits, under O.C.G.A. § 5-4-1. The petition must be filed within 30 days of the final written decision. The Superior Court does not retry the case; instead, it reviews the record for legal error, lack of evidence, or arbitrary action. Constitutional challenges (such as takings or due process claims) may also be raised, but Georgia courts require strict procedural compliance, including raising the issue before the local board first. Failing to preserve an objection at the hearing usually waives it on appeal.

How a Demand Letter Works in Georgia

A Georgia zoning variance appeal letter serves three purposes: it puts the local government on formal notice of your legal objections, it preserves issues for judicial review, and it often opens the door to settlement before certiorari is filed. The letter should be sent to the BZA chair, the zoning administrator, the county or city attorney, and—when applicable—the applicant whose variance is being challenged.

A strong letter cites the specific provisions of the local zoning ordinance the board misapplied, identifies which of the four Georgia hardship factors were not properly analyzed, and references O.C.G.A. § 36-66-5 (requiring written standards and procedures). If the board failed to make findings of fact, ignored evidence, or relied on impermissible factors (such as neighborhood opposition alone, which Georgia courts have repeatedly held insufficient), the letter should say so plainly.

Include a clear demand: rescission of the decision, a rehearing, modification of conditions, or notice that you intend to file a writ of certiorari within the 30-day window. Attach exhibits—survey plats, photographs, hearing transcripts, and prior board decisions showing inconsistent treatment. A well-documented letter often prompts the local government's attorney to recommend a voluntary rehearing rather than defend a weak record in Superior Court. Even when the letter does not produce a reversal, it strengthens your certiorari petition by showing the court you tried to resolve the matter administratively and that the local board had a fair chance to correct its error.

Procedural Notes for Georgia

In Georgia, a petition for writ of certiorari must be filed in the Superior Court of the county where the property is located within 30 days of the BZA's written decision. Filing fees vary by county but typically range from $200 to $230. A bond may be required under O.C.G.A. § 5-4-5. Small claims (magistrate) court, with its $15,000 limit, generally cannot hear zoning appeals—these are equitable and administrative matters reserved for Superior Court. Service must be made on the local governing authority and any interested party. Strict compliance with notice and hearing requirements at the local level is essential; issues not raised below are typically waived. Consult local rules, as several Georgia counties have supplemental procedures.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to appeal a Georgia zoning variance decision?
You have 30 days from the date of the Board of Zoning Appeals' final written decision to file a petition for writ of certiorari in Superior Court under O.C.G.A. § 5-4-1. This deadline is jurisdictional, meaning courts have no power to extend it. Sending an appeal letter before that deadline preserves your position and may prompt a voluntary rehearing. Missing the 30-day window almost always ends your right to challenge the decision in court.
Can I challenge a variance my neighbor received in Georgia?
Yes, if you have legal standing. Georgia courts require that you be an "aggrieved party"—typically an adjacent or nearby property owner who can show a specific, substantial injury different from the general public, such as decreased property value, loss of light or air, increased traffic, or drainage problems. You must have participated in the BZA hearing and raised your objections on the record. A formal appeal letter documents your standing and preserves the issues for certiorari review.
What must I prove to win a Georgia variance appeal?
On certiorari review, the Superior Court asks whether the BZA's decision was supported by any evidence, was within its legal authority, and followed required procedures. You generally must show the board misapplied the four-factor hardship test, ignored evidence, made no written findings, relied on improper factors like neighborhood opposition alone, or violated due process. Constitutional claims like takings require additional proof. The court will not substitute its judgment unless the decision was arbitrary or legally erroneous.
Do I need a lawyer to send a zoning appeal letter in Georgia?
No, property owners can send appeal letters themselves, and a well-drafted letter often resolves disputes without litigation. However, the actual writ of certiorari filing in Superior Court involves complex procedural rules, evidentiary records, and constitutional issues where legal representation is strongly recommended. Many Georgia attorneys handle zoning matters on flat fees or hourly rates. Sending the letter yourself first can narrow the issues and demonstrate good faith if the case proceeds to court.
Can attorney's fees be recovered in a Georgia zoning appeal?
Generally, each side bears its own fees in zoning disputes. However, under O.C.G.A. § 9-15-14, fees may be awarded if the local government's position was substantially frivolous, groundless, or asserted in bad faith. Some local ordinances or constitutional violations (such as 42 U.S.C. § 1983 due process claims) may also support fee awards. Recovery is the exception, not the rule, so plan for legal costs. A strong appeal letter that produces an early settlement is often the most cost-effective remedy.
