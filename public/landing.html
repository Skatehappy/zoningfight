What Is ZoningFight?

The fastest way to write a zoning variance appeal letter.

A zoning variance is a formal request to deviate from your municipality's zoning code — whether that means building closer to a property line than allowed (a setback variance), using land in a way that doesn't conform to current zoning (a use variance), or exceeding height, lot coverage, or square footage limits (a dimensional variance). Winning a variance requires a written appeal letter that directly addresses your local zoning board's specific evaluation criteria.

ZoningFight is an AI-powered zoning variance letter generator that produces professional, attorney-quality appeal letters in under 5 minutes. You describe your situation in plain English, paste in your town's evaluation criteria, and the AI writes a complete letter — citing applicable state statutes, addressing every required criterion, and making the strongest possible legal argument for your hardship.

Who uses ZoningFight?

Homeowners who need a setback variance for a new garage, deck, addition, or fence. Property owners seeking a use variance to run a home business or build an accessory dwelling unit. Developers and contractors facing dimensional variance requirements on a tight site. Anyone who has received a notice of zoning violation and needs to appeal. If you're facing a zoning board hearing and need a compelling written argument, ZoningFight gets you there without paying attorney fees.

Why does the letter matter?

Zoning boards are quasi-judicial bodies — they follow formal procedures, keep written records, and are legally required to evaluate every application against specific written criteria. A verbal argument at a hearing is rarely enough. Boards want to see a written submission that directly addresses their standards, establishes legal hardship, demonstrates that approval serves the public interest, and documents that the variance is the minimum necessary relief. A well-written variance letter is the difference between approval and denial.

Setback, use, or dimensional variance denied. The letter addresses every board criterion, documents hardship, and shows the requested relief is the minimum variance necessary.

Building Permit Denial
Municipality denied the permit without citing specific code sections, or applied standards inconsistently compared to similar nearby approvals. Due process requires specificity.
🛑
Stop-Work / Cease and Desist
Order issued against permitted activity. Vested rights, non-conforming use protections, and due process demand suspension pending administrative review.
🏛️
Non-Conforming Use Dispute
Municipality attempting to eliminate your pre-existing legal use. Vested non-conforming status is a constitutional property right; amortization must be reasonable.
📋
Conditional Use Permit Denial
CUP denied without required findings. Unlike variances, CUPs are for uses already deemed appropriate — the board's role is to verify enumerated criteria, not re-evaluate policy.
🔍
Code Interpretation Dispute
Municipality reading the ordinance against you. Rules of statutory construction: ambiguity is construed in favor of the property owner against the government.
🪧
Sign Permit Denial
Content-based sign restrictions denied or selectively enforced. Reed v. Town of Gilbert makes content-based sign codes presumptively unconstitutional.
⚖️
Code Enforcement / Neighbor Complaint
Notice issued for activity you believe is permitted, or selectively enforced against you. Class-of-one equal protection under Village of Willowbrook v. Olech applies.

Does ZoningFight work for my town?
Yes. You paste your town's variance evaluation criteria directly into the form — the AI addresses each criterion explicitly in your letter. ZoningFight works for every municipality in all 50 states because it uses your board's own standards, not a generic template.
What is the difference between Standard and Assertive versions?
Standard is measured and professional — appropriate for most boards. Assertive is more forceful and direct — better when you have strong precedents, a clear-cut hardship, or a board that has been unresponsive. You get both versions and choose which to submit.
Do I need a surveyor or site plan?
The letter references your measurements but does not create site plans. Most zoning boards require a basic site plan with your application. The included submission checklist walks you through everything required beyond the letter itself.
